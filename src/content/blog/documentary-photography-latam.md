The first time I flew into Lima on assignment for a European magazine, I arrived with two bodies, four lenses, and the confident ignorance of someone who had done a lot of research but not nearly enough listening.

The editor wanted images of poverty. The city gave me something more complicated — a place of extraordinary energy, grinding inequality, and a visual richness that resisted every easy narrative I had prepared.

## The political act of the frame

Every photograph is a choice. What you include, what you exclude, where you stand, what focal length flattens or compresses the relationship between subject and background — all of it is editorial. In documentary photography, pretending otherwise is a form of dishonesty.

Working for publications like The Guardian and The New York Times means understanding that your images will be placed next to text, inside a narrative someone else has written. The responsibility is to make images that can hold their own interpretation — that resist being reduced to illustration.

## What a decade in Latin America taught me about access

Trust is slow. It is built in the spaces between assignments — in the willingness to come back, to follow up, to learn enough Spanish to have a conversation that is not about photography. The images that have meant the most to me were made with people I had known for at least two visits.

Extraction — fly in, shoot, fly out — produces a particular kind of image. It is technically competent and emotionally thin. The publications buy it, and the audience moves past it in three seconds.

## On the Picture of the Year recognition

Winning at Picture of the Year Latam mattered to me not because of the prize, but because of what it represented — recognition from within the region, by editors and photographers who understand the specific complexity of representing Latin America to a global audience.

The winning series was made over fourteen months, across four countries. No single assignment. A personal project carried between paid work. That, more than any trophy, is what documentary photography requires.
