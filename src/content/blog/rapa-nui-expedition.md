There is a moment on the five-hour flight from Santiago to Rapa Nui — somewhere over the open Pacific, no land visible in any direction — where the isolation of where you are going becomes real. The island is a pinprick in the largest ocean on earth. Everything that arrives there has been carried a very long way.

The film — *Landed*, commissioned for a luxury travel brand — was supposed to be a three-day shoot. It became five, because Rapa Nui operates on its own schedule and the late afternoon light on the Ahu Tongariki moai simply cannot be rushed.

## Logistics at the end of the world

Every piece of equipment you bring to Rapa Nui is equipment you are responsible for repairing on-island if it breaks, because there is no next-day courier service, no rental house, no backup. The discipline this enforces is, in retrospect, one of the best things that can happen to a filmmaker's kit selection.

I brought what I needed and nothing I didn't. The drone — essential for the aerial perspectives that give the island's volcanic coastline its proper scale — was triple-packed and carried as hand luggage.

## The moai in the early morning

Every photographer who visits Rapa Nui photographs the moai. The challenge is to find an angle that does not look like every other photograph of the moai. The answer, as it usually is, was time — specifically, being at Ahu Tongariki at 5:45 in the morning, before any other visitor arrived, in the twenty minutes when the eastern sky goes from black to a deep bruised violet.

The shot that opened the final film was made in that window. We waited four mornings for the right cloud formation.

## What the island teaches

Rapa Nui is a place that forces slowness. There is nowhere to rush to. The internet is unreliable. The single main road circles the island in forty minutes. What remains, once the urgency drains away, is a quality of attention that makes better images.

I came back with 800 gigabytes of footage. The film is eleven minutes long. That ratio — that willingness to capture everything and use almost nothing — is the only way to make something that is actually worth watching.
