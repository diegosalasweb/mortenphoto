When the Google project came in, it looked like most other briefs on the surface. A brand with a story to tell, a budget, a timeline, and a list of key messages that needed to land. What made it different was the client's willingness to get out of the way.

The Concha y Toro story — a Chilean winery with a history that predated most of the brands Google works with — needed to be told slowly. It needed wine, and soil, and the specific quality of afternoon light in the Maipo Valley. It did not need a logo at the end.

## The brief as a starting point, not a ceiling

The best branded content I have made has always started the same way: by ignoring the brief for the first meeting. Not permanently. But long enough to ask the question that the brief never asks — what does the *audience* actually want to watch?

People do not watch advertising. They watch stories. The moment a branded film starts feeling like an advertisement, viewers disengage. The moment it stops feeling like one, you have their full attention.

## Craft is not optional

One thing Cannes judges talk about consistently is production quality. Not in a superficial sense, but in the sense that craft communicates respect — for the subject, for the audience, for the brand. Shaky footage and mediocre colour grading say something about what a company thinks of its audience.

The Concha y Toro film was colour graded for three days. We pulled a LUT that referenced the warm, slightly desaturated palette of 1970s Chilean documentary photography. Nothing about that decision was in the brief. Everything about it was right for the story.

## What the Gold Lion actually means

Recognition is useful — it opens doors, it gives clients confidence, it helps justify production budgets. But it is a lagging indicator, not a leading one. The work that wins was made correctly long before anyone in Cannes saw it.

Make it for the audience first. The awards follow.
