The Atacama does not care about your schedule. It does not care about your camera, your hard drives, or the deadline you negotiated with the client six months ago. What it cares about — if a desert can care about anything — is patience.

I arrived in San Pedro with a Sony FX6, a single assistant, and what I thought was a comprehensive pre-production plan. By day three, the dust had found its way into everything. By day seven, I had thrown the plan out entirely.

## What the desert strips away

Filming in extreme environments teaches you to work with less. Not because you want to, but because the location demands it. Heavy rigs become liabilities. Large crews become logistical nightmares. The Atacama, sitting at 2,400 metres above sea level with almost zero humidity, punished every shortcut and rewarded simplicity.

The images that made it into the final cut — the ones that actually moved people — were almost all handheld, shot in the early morning or the last twenty minutes of golden hour. The elaborate stabiliser setups we brought? Used on maybe four shots.

## The technical reality

Dust is your primary enemy. Even in a sealed Pelican case, fine Atacama particles find microscopic entry points. I cleaned my sensor every single day. I lost one ND filter to scratching damage in the first week. Battery life dropped noticeably at altitude — plan for 20–30% less capacity than sea level performance.

The upside is the light. There is nowhere on earth with light like the Atacama at sunrise. The clarity of the atmosphere, the way the pink and orange reflects off the salt flats — it is the kind of light that makes any camera look like a more expensive one.

## What I'd do differently

Bring more cards, fewer lenses. The temptation is to pack every focal length you own. Resist it. I shot 80% of the documentary on the 35mm and the 70–200. Everything else was weight I carried for nothing.

Most importantly: build two extra days into any Atacama shoot. The desert will take at least one of them.
